The nice thing about this trip

Both times you change planes, it's at the same airport — Toronto Pearson, Terminal 1 — and you never even leave that one building. You learn your way around it once on the way there, and it's already familiar on the way home.

Free WiFi with movies and shows you can stream to your own phone or tablet — but no seat-back screen and no power outlet, so come with a charged phone and a book.

A comfortable midday start — no early alarm. Winnipeg has one terminal and about twenty gates, so whatever gate you need is a short walk from the front door. There's no rush — get there in good time and sit down with a coffee.

No border here

This flight goes to Toronto, which is still Canada. So Winnipeg is just ordinary domestic security — bag on the belt, walk through, done. No passport officers, no customs, no American anything. That part comes later, in Toronto, and this guide walks you through every step of it.

Say hello

You'll find out your seat when you get to the gate, so you won't know who you're sitting beside until you're on board. Take it as a treat — a long, easy afternoon is the perfect time to meet a new friend or two. Bring your smile.

2
Wednesday afternoon · Toronto Pearson

Toronto, on the way there. This is where the border happens — and you have plenty of time.

You have 1 hour and 40 minutes here, which is comfortably more than enough. This is the one stretch of the trip where you'll do the American border — but unlike a tight connection, you're not racing. Walk it at your own pace.

  • ~10 minGet off the plane and follow the purple Connections signs toward U.S. departures. No rush — take your time.
  • ~15 minSecurity screening — bag on the belt, same as Winnipeg.
  • ~40 minU.S. border officers. Passport, and a couple of easy questions — where you're going, how long, why. Just answer plainly: visiting family in New York for six days.
  • ~10 minWalk to your gate.
  • ~25 minTime to spare before boarding. Sit down — the hard part is behind you.

About 1 h 40 in all — land 15:45, board 17:25. These are rough estimates; the border line is the only real variable.

If you're ever unsure — just ask

You never have to work anything out on your own. Anyone in an airport uniform will happily point you the right way, and you don't even need to explain yourself — just show your boarding pass and say:

"Hello — I'm connecting to flight AC 718 to New York. Am I going the right way?"

That's the whole trick. The signs are clear too, and with an hour and forty minutes you can stop and ask as many people as you like.

Gates aren't assigned until a day or two before the flight, so nobody can tell you a gate number today — not even Air Canada. It'll be in the app and on the screens when you land. That's normal, and it's not something to worry about.

5
Tuesday afternoon · Toronto Pearson

Toronto again — the gentle version. Follow the purple.

Same airport you already know from Wednesday, so nothing here is new. You have an hour and a half, and coming back into Canada is much quicker than going into America was.

  • ~10 minOff the plane, look up and follow the purple signs — down the escalator toward Canada Customs.
  • ~25 minCanada Customs. A kiosk — scan your passport, a few taps — then a Canadian officer says hello. You're coming home; this is the easiest checkpoint of the trip.
  • ~15 minKeep following purple to domestic departures and your Winnipeg gate (the D gates). Washrooms and coffee along the way.
  • ~40 minTime to spare before boarding. Plenty of time to sit.

About 1 h 30 in all — land 13:10, board 14:40. These are rough estimates.

And the same trick works here — show your boarding pass to anyone in uniform and ask:

"I'm connecting to flight AC 265 to Winnipeg. Am I going the right way?"

One useful signal: if you ever end up on a platform waiting for a little train, you've wandered off course. That train goes to a different terminal and you never need it — your whole visit to Toronto happens inside Terminal 1, both times.

Finding your way in Toronto

Both times you change planes, it's inside Terminal 1 — one building. Picture it as two ends with the border in the middle. Whichever way you're headed, follow the purple “Connections” signs and they carry you the whole way.

The other two airports are the easy ones. Winnipeg is a single small terminal — your gate is a short walk from check-in, nothing to find. New York is LaGuardia's Terminal B both ways: arriving, you walk straight off the plane and out; leaving, it's an ordinary security line, then your gate.

ii In Toronto, follow the purple signs. Purple means connections — both on the way there and on the way home. Follow it and it walks you to the right place every time.

Before the day

  • Passport — somewhere easy to reach, like your handbag.
  • A book and a charged phone — the first flight has no seat-back screen.
  • Check in — opens 24 hours before. Steve can do it from New York.
  • Arrive early — there's no prize for cutting it close.

Four flights, all on one ticket with one airline. That last part matters: if anything anywhere runs late, Air Canada owns the problem and puts you on the next flight. You will never be asked to sort it out yourself.

Gate numbers are assigned close to the day of travel and aren't published in advance — check the Air Canada app or the airport screens on the day. Your booking reference is in the confirmation email; it's deliberately not printed on this page.
